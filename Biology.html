Transpiration
It is the loss of water vapor from leaves due to evaporation and diffusion.

How?
1. Water evaporates from the mesophyll cell walls into the air spaces within the leaf.
2. The water vapor then diffuses through the stomata (small openings on the leaf surface) down a concentration gradient, which is from an area of high concentration (inside the leaf) to an area of low concentration (outside the leaf).

Cohesion → the attraction between molecules of the same substance (e.g. water molecule with another water molecule)
Adhesion → the attraction between different molecules (e.g water molecules and the cellulose in the cell walls of the xylem)

This sticking force helps the water fight gravity and climb up from the roots to the leaves. While cohesion keeps the water molecules together in a continuous column, adhesion keeps that column anchored to the walls so it doesn't slip back down.

Factors
The transpiration rate is affected by several factors, including light intensity, temperature, humidity, wind speed and stomata availability.

-light intensity → Light causes the stomata to open wider which allows for a greater diffusion of water vapor out of the leaf. Due to that, it can increase the rate of water uptake through the xylem.
-Temperature and humidity → Higher temperatures increase the rate of transpiration by providing more kinetic energy to water molecules, making them evaporate and diffuse faster. A higher humidity means the air is already saturated with water, so it reduces the concentration gradient and slows down water loss from the leaf.
-Wind speed → Increased air movement carries away the humid layer of air directly around the leaf, replacing it with drier air and increase the transpiration rate.
Stomata availability → When more stomata opens, water vapor can easily diffuse out of the leaf, increasing transpiration. When stomata are closed, water loss is reduced, decreasing transpiration.

Plant growth by transpiration
An increased transpiration leads to increased water uptake creating a "pull" that draws water up from the roots through the xylem. This continuous water flow is essential for the plant to obtain the water it needs.

Water is essential for processes like photosynthesis, maintaining turgor pressure (keeping cells firm), and cell elongation (which is how plants grow taller).
● Photosynthesis: the process in which plants convert light energy into chemical energy (sugars). Adequate water supply ensures that photosynthesis can occur efficiently.
● Turgor pressure: water inside plant cells creates turgor pressure, pushing the cell membrane against the cell wall. It helps keep the cells firm and the plant upright.
● Cell elongation: turgor pressure is also essential for cell elongation, which is how plants grow taller and larger. As cells take up water, they expand, contributing to the plant's overall growth.

In addition, increased transpiration, facilitated by open stomata, drives water uptake, which is vital for photosynthesis, maintaining cell firmness, and plant growth. However, if transpiration occurs too rapidly, the plant can lose water faster than it can be replaced, leading to wilting and other problems.
→ Plant growth can also be significantly influenced by light intensity. Higher light intensity generally increases photosynthesis, providing more energy and better growth.
More stomata opens to increase transpiration rate, directly supporting the mass flow of nutrients to growing tissues, supporting rapid height increase.

Metabolic Processes in Mung Bean (Vigna radiata)

-Cation Exchange in the Soil: Mung bean roots facilitate this process through cation exchange. During respiration, roots produce carbon dioxide (CO₂), which reacts with water in the soil to form carbonic acid (H₂CO₃). This weak acid dissociates into hydrogen ions (H⁺). The roots actively release these H⁺ ions into the soil, where they displace the nutrient cations attached to soil particles. As a result, the nutrients are freed into the soil solution and can be absorbed by the plant roots.
-Metabolic Processes in the Plant: water and nutrients are absorbed and transported to the leaves, the plant carries out metabolic reactions that support growth and survival.
i. Photosynthesis (Anabolism): Photosynthesis is an anabolic process in which the plant converts light energy into chemical energy stored in glucose.
6CO2 + 6H2O -> C6H12O6 + 6O2
This process occurs in the chloroplasts of leaf cells and produces glucose, which serves as an energy source, and oxygen, which is released into the atmosphere.
ii. Cellular Respiration (Catabolism): Cellular respiration is a catabolic process that breaks down glucose to release energy in the form of ATP.
C6​H12​O6​+6O2​→6CO2​+6H2​O+ATP
ATP generated through respiration is used to power essential activities such as growth, cell division, and active transport processes like nutrient uptake.

Rate of Reactions & Decomposition
How fast chemical processes happen in plants, like photosynthesis and respiration. Faster rates = faster growth and energy production.The breakdown of dead plant material by microorganisms, releasing nutrients back into the soil for new plants to use.

Collision Theory and Enzyme Activity: The rate of metabolic reactions in mung beans can be explained by collision theory, which states that reactions occur when particles collide with sufficient energy. At room temperature, molecules possess moderate kinetic energy, allowing enzymes to function efficiently without being denatured.
Decomposition of Fertilizers:Fertilizers often contain organic nitrogen compounds such as urea. Before plants can absorb nitrogen, it must undergo decomposition. This occurs through hydrolysis, catalyzed by the enzyme urease =
(NH2​)2​CO+H2​O→2NH3​+CO2
The ammonia (NH₃) produced then reacts with water to form ammonium ions:
NH3​+H2​O→NH4+​+OH−
Ammonium (NH₄⁺) is the form of nitrogen that can be readily absorbed by plant roots.
Factors Affecting Reaction Rates: -Temperature = At 20–25°C, enzymatic reactions proceed at an optimal rate. Lower temperatures reduce molecular movement and slow reactions, while excessively high temperatures can denature enzymes.
-Water Availability = Water acts as a solvent and medium for chemical reactions. It also facilitates nutrient transport.

Essential Nutrients (NPK)
Nitrogen,Phosphorus,Potassium

-Nitrogen (N): Essential for chlorophyll production and protein synthesis. Although mung beans can fix atmospheric nitrogen through symbiotic bacteria in root nodules, they still rely on soil nitrogen during early growth stages.
-Phosphorus (P): Plays a crucial role in energy transfer through ATP and supports root development.
-Potassium (K): Regulates stomatal opening and closing, helping to control water balance and prevent dehydration.

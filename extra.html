PH

-Our soil pH ranges from 7-7.5.
- The pH of the soil actually affects nutrient absorption by influencing the solubility and availability of nutrients in the soil.
-Different nutrients are most available at different pH levels. For example, some nutrients are more soluble and readily absorbed by plants in acidic conditions, while others are more available in alkaline conditions. When the pH is outside the optimal range for a particular nutrient, it can become less soluble, and plants may not be able to absorb it effectively, leading to nutrient deficiencies.
-Mung beans grow best in well-draining sandy loam soil with a pH level between 6.2 and 7.2. While they prefer slightly acidic to neutral conditions, they can tolerate a range, but performance drops in strongly acidic (below 5) or highly alkaline (above 8-9) soils, which can restrict growth.

Effects of resistance on brightness
Ohm’s Law (V=IR) states that current is directly proportional to the potential difference across its ends and inversely proportional to resistance.

A component that obeys Ohm's law is known as an ohmic conductor (e.g. fixed resistor):
-The I–V graph is a straight line through the origin
For a non-ohmic conductor, such as:
-A semiconductor diode, the I–V graph is a horizontal line that goes sharply upwards. A diode is used in a circuit to allow current to flow only in a specific direction.
-An LED (light-emitting diode), the I–V graph is similar to the semiconductor diode, since it is a semiconductor device that emits light when an electric current flows through it.
-A filament lamp, the I–V graph has an 'S' shaped curve.As the current increases, the temperature of the filament in the lamp increases. Since the filament is a metal, the higher temperature causes an increase in resistance.
-A thermistor, the I–V graph is a curved line with increasing gradient through the origin. The increase in the potential difference results in an increase in current which causes the temperature of the thermistor to rise, decreasing resistance and this means even more current is able to flow through.

Effect of resistance on brightness
The brightness of a light source (e.g. light bulb) is directly related to the electrical power (P) it consumes. → Power is calculated as P = VI, where V is voltage and I is current. Since V = IR, we can also express power as P = I^2 * R or P = V^2 / R.

How does it derive? P = I^2 * R or P = V^2 / R..?
So what's the effect of resistance on brightness?
→ Increasing Resistance: If you increase the resistance in a circuit (while keeping the voltage constant), the current decreases. Since power (P) is proportional to the square of current (I^2), a decrease in current leads to a decrease in power. Less power means the light source will be dimmer.
→ Decreasing Resistance: If you decrease the resistance in a circuit (while keeping the voltage constant), the current increases. This increase in current leads to an increase in power. More power means the light source will be brighter.
How power and light intensity are connected?
→ Power is the rate at which energy is used or produced. In the case of a light source, power represents the rate at which it converts energy (like electrical energy) into light energy.
→ Light intensity, often measured in lumens or candelas, is a measure of the amount of light emitted by a source. It tells us how "bright" the light appears.
More Power = More Light: When a light source uses more power, it has more energy available to convert into light. This results in a higher rate of light emission, leading to brighter appearance
